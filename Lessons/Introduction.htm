Introduction to Neural Networks in Cognitive Science

Cognitive Science

Neural networks, which simulate brain circuits, are used in a wide variety of contexts for wide variety of reasons. Engineers use them, chemists use them, psychologists use them, and so forth. The focus here is on the way neural networks are used in cognitive science. Since we focus on neural networks in cognitive science, let us begin by saying what cognitive science is.

Cognitive science is the interdisciplinary and multi-disciplinary study of the mind. It draws on such diverse fields as psychology, linguistics, computer science, neuroscience, philosophy, anthropology, and others. Not all these people are cognitive scientists, but there are some people in each of those fields who do work in cognitive science.

What these people have in common is that they want to know how the mind works. The mind really is remarkable. Let’s take an example. When I go to the store and buy a loaf of bread, I perceive cans and boxes, and vegetables and other things. I recognize these as boxes, cans, vegetables and so forth. I successfully control my body in a complex environment: I am able to push a shopping cart around other shoppers who hurtle by and retrieve loaves of bread from crowded shelves. I remember what I am supposed to buy and plan ahead for dinner that night and all week. I easily find the things I need because I’ve been to this store before and have learned about its spatial layout. I see someone I know in the store and talk to him briefly.

That I can do all this so well is remarkable. You may not be impressed but you’re another human. Try to build a robot to do one or two these things, let alone all of them, as quickly and effortlessly as we humans do. And we do it using this wet hunk of organic material in our skulls. Millions of firing neurons somehow allow us to perceive, recognized, control, remember, plan, learn, and talk all day every day. How on earth does this work? That’s what cognitive scientists are trying to figure out.

This may seem like psychology to you. And it is. Psychologists study this stuff by putting people in controlled experimental situations and recording what they do. For the most part they study behavior: if we show people these stimuli, how will they respond? But the behavioral data is just part of the story. There are other ways to try to understand how I did so well at getting that bread. Computer scientists and engineers write computer programs or build robots which try to do all the things we do. Neuroscientists study the brain by measuring its activity, in an effort to understand the physical mechanisms which allow us to behave so successfully in the world. Linguistics study language by, for example, studying sets of sentences which are and are not considered grammatical and proposing theories which explain the difference. Philosophers look at all this data and, especially in cases where experimental data is not available, attempt to interpret it and synthesize it into general theories of how the mind and brain work.

Information Processing

So cognitive science is the interdisciplinary study of the mind. There are many specific ways to spin this; there are many particular ways of studying the mind and brain. What cognitive scientists tend to share is a focus on information processing in the mind and brain.

Although each discipline has its own unique interpretation of cognitive science, they are bound into a cohesive whole by a central tenet. This tenet states that the mind is an information processor; that is, it receives, stores, retrieves, transforms, and transmits information" ([105], p. 1). Medler, quoting Aizawa.

It may help to consider some historical context here. Cognitive science is often traced to one of several conferences in the 1950’s, including in particular a conference held at MIT in 1956, the Symposium on Information Theory:

Three talks in particular, Miller's The magical number seven, Chomsky's Three models of language, and Newell and Simon's Logic theory machine, have been singled out as instrumental in seeding the cognitive science movement. Following these talks, a perception began to emerge that “human experimental psychology, theoretical linguistics, and computer simulations of cognitive processes were all pieces of a larger whole" (Miller, 1979; p. 9; cited in [36], p. 29).

The big idea, that idea that got everyone excited, was based on the observation that the mind and brain are like a computer in that they takes in information (in the form of sensory processes), process it internally, and then produce output (in the form of behavioral response).

 

 

So, the new generation thought, the mind itself is an information processing device, and we can use computers and mathematics together with psychology and neuroscience and all the rest to understand the way it processes information. This was in part a response to the older behaviorism, which said you shouldn’t look “inside the mind at all,” and just look at inputs and outputs. The new cognitive scientists wanted to break open the black box and look inside at the guts: the processing the mind and brain do. And they had these new devices, electronics computers, to help them do it.

This new breed of inter-disciplinary scientists would split according to how they thought the mind processes information. Some thought (and think) the mind processes information using symbols and rules, like a regular computer does. This remains the dominant tenedency in cognitive science, and is sometimes called the “rules and representations” or “symbolic AI” approach. The other camp thought the mind processes information more like the brain does. We will focus on the second camp, the “connectionists.” Today there are other camps, and some who mix ideas from both symbolic AI and connectionism. More on this later. For now let’s look at neural networks themselves.

Neural Networks: A first pass

So cognitive science is the interdisciplinary study of mind with an emphasis on information processing. People who study neural networks are interested in brain-like processing as the foundation of thinking. How is it that something like a set of neurons connected by synapses could allow me to go buy my loaf of bread and engage in intelligent conversation and all the rest?

We are now in a position to give a first-pass definition of a neural network. A neural network is a collection of neuron-like units connected by weights which interacts with an environment (there are ways of referring to these units and weights, noted below). A neural network can be pictured like this:

There are different ways of drawing neural networks, but they generally look something like the above, which is a screenshot from Simbrain. There are a few things to focus on in this diagram:

1) The open white circles. These are nodes or artificial neurons, which process information which comes into them and then pass it along.

2) The lines which connect the circles together, with blue and red discs at the end of them. These are artificial synapses or weights. They represent the flow of information in a neural network. They can have different values associated with them, and those values can change over time. As these connections change the way the neural network processes information changes.

3) The arrows pointing in at the bottom and pointing out at the top. These correspond to sensory inputs at the bottom, which receive stimuli from the environment, and motor outputs, which cause a change in the environment. These are also called input nodes and output nodes. We will see that these can be connected or coupled to an environment in many different ways.

In other lessons there is more detail about how these different components work. For now just notice that all the pieces of the basic information processing diagram above are here: the network takes in inputs, deals with them, and then produces outputs. And this is no black box, as the behaviorists would have had it. We have opened the black box and are looking inside at how the processing occurs.

In the first example below, you will open up a simple network like this one and see the way it channels information. In the second example you will see how a simple network can actually control the behavior of an agent.

Engineering vs. Cognitive Modeling

The field of neural networks can be broken up according to dimensions or spectra. First, one can classify neural network research to whether the person building the network is interested in how human or animal minds work or whether they are mainly trying to use the neural network to solve a problem. Let’s call this the engineering / modeling spectrum:

 

At one end are the engineers. For them, neural networks are tools which solve problems. It turns out that neural networks are good at doing certain kinds of thing, like finding patterns in noisy data, controlling certain kinds of machine (e.g. the neuro-fuzzy rice cooker), and in general dealing with problems that involves lots of variables that are hard to deal with explicitly. People who do that don’t care about how the brain works or how we think: they want to make a machine that works. That rice cooker better make good rice, whether it thinks right or not.

At the other hand are the modelers, the ones who want to know how the mind works. For them, neural networks are models of brain processes or mental proceses or both (see the next dimension). These modelers aren’t trying to solve engineering problems, they are trying to understand the way the human mind works. How do we process inputs and turn them into outputs and manipulate them in the middle? For someone at that end of the spectrum errors are good. Humans make errors. We can’t add big numbers all too well. A cheap calculator kicks our butt on that one. The engineers have that mastered, and it’s clearly not the same as a human. But if we could make a neural network which added and had the same trouble we do, that would be interesting. It would tell us something about how our minds and brains solve that particular problem. So, we’ve got a kind of continuum (it’s really more discrete, like an on-off switch), but there are a variety of commitments to engineering or cognitive modeling respectively.

In what follows we will mention engineering work, especially in the discussion of backpropagation, but our focus will be on the cognitive modeling end of the spectrum.

Connectionism vs. Computational Neuroscience

Now for the second continuum. Let’s call this one a continuum between connectionism and computational neuroscience.

At one end of the spectrum are people who use neuron-like elements to study the mind. Their models often simulate the mind in a manner which is plausibly brain-like, but not directly brain like. There is no suggestion, for example, that each unit is like a real neuron. These people are showing how something like a brain could do human type stuff. The goal is more to capture psychological data than biological data.

At the other end are people who are mainly modeling the brain. They build models of individual neurons, often taking into account all kinds of details about how individuals neurons are wired up. This is often called computational neuroscience. For them neurons really are like neurons.

This one really is a spectrum. There are people for example who link up thousands of realistic neurons but without actually trying to capture any specific brain system.

Nomenclature

Much of the terminology in the field of neural networks is overloaded. Often, the different terms are associated with different positions on the dimensions above.

“Connectionist networks” go by other names. They are also referred to as “neural networks,” “artificial neural networks,” and “parallel distributed processing” or “pdp” systems.

“Neurons” are also called “artificial neurons,” “units” or “nodes.” The “artificial” is useful because connectionist networks are never real neurons. Nonetheless, it is convenient to refer to these as neurons, so we sometimes will do so.

“Synapses” are also called “weights” or “connections.”

Modeling in general

Computers are useful in studying the brain because the brain is difficult to study directly. Consider. It’s relatively easy to study a cabbage. It is easily prodded. Its internal structure is pretty regular. It has no emotions (well, arguably not—but that is a philosophical question) so we can chop it and cut it at will. And cabbage chunks are easily inspected with a microscope. Now contrast a live brain. It is about the size, in humans, of a small cabbage, yes, but there the differences end. Brains have incredibly complex internal structure. Living brains “have” or at least produce feelings like pain, so we ought to be careful in our cuttings and proddings. And even if we do manage to stick in an electrode without violating ethical principles, all we get is high-level, summary information about what lots and lots of neurons are doing.

For these reasons, computers help. A computer simulation of a brain feels no pain (again, arguably; that is also a philosophical question and a much hotter one than the one about cabbages), so we can play with it as we wish. And the computer simulation is accessible. We can zoom in and out on simulated neurons, change their simulated firing rates, and see what happens in great detail, without disturbing any live tissue.

It is important to note that models make simplifying assumptions. Thus, any particular model of the brain will leave many things out. This is fine, as long as one is aware of them.  In order to get a simulation off the ground certain aspects of the reality in question have to be ignored.  The effects of mitochondria, glial cells, and a whole range of cellular machinery are left out of the connectionist equations overviewed in sections following.   Forgetting this point can be disastrous.  But if kept firmly in mind, simplifying assumptions are harmless and even helpful (cf the maxims of empirical science in 3.3).
